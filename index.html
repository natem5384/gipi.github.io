About the GIPI

The Green Infrastructure Potential Index (GIPI) is a geospatial composite index developed for Nate Mestre’s 2026 master’s thesis at NYU. It uses multi-criteria decision analysis to integrate high-resolution data on flooding risk, extreme heat, and air pollution, ranking urban areas by their potential for introducing environmentally sustainable blue and green infrastructure (BGI; bioswales, green roofs, urban tree canopy, permeable pavement, etc.). This interactive map only reflects city-wide GIPI averages and inter-city comparison (benchmarking municipalities against one another) product of the thesis. The other product of the thesis, intra-city GIPI mapping , that is, mapping where BGI should best be implemented within a city, is not included in this map. Ultimately, the project aims at assessing where US cities lie in their potential for BGI introduction,helping planners move beyond outdated gray infrastructure toward more resilient, sustainable urban environments adapted for a changing world
Hydrological Component (HC)
Eq. 1
HC = 0.7 · √(Imperv × Precip) + 0.1 · CSO + 0.2 · Prkng
  • Imperv — Impervious surface fraction of the city (roads, rooftops, sidewalks). Higher values mean more runoff.
  • Precip — Precipitation estimate for a 2-year, 1-hour design storm (inches). Captures acute flood risk.
  • CSO — Combined Sewer Overflow presence in the city. Cities with combined sewers can discharge raw sewage during heavy rain.
  • Prkng — Parking lot area as a share of the city’s land. Large impervious lots are prime candidates for green retrofit.
The dominant sub-component uses the geometric mean of imperviousness and precipitation (square-root product), reflecting how flood risk emerges from the interaction of impervious cover and storm intensity rather than either factor alone.
Heat Severity Component (HS)
Eq. 2
HS = 0.5 · HeatGeo + 0.5 · AugT
  • HeatGeo — Urban Heat Island (UHI) intensity derived from land-surface temperature differentials. Measures how much hotter a location is relative to its non-urban surroundings. The baseline temperature is excluded so that the metric captures the UHI effect alone.
  • AugT — August nighttime low temperature. Captures dangerous overnight heat exposure that prevents the human body from recovering, which is a key driver of heat-related mortality.
Both sub-components are weighted equally. HeatGeo isolates the local urban-heat amplification, while AugT anchors results to actual observed extreme temperatures, ensuring cities in hotter climates are appropriately flagged.
Air Quality Component (AQ)
The Air Quality component is included as a raw dataset (no internal sub-component aggregation). It comes from a CDC environmental justice dataset and is a percentile rank consisting of # of exceedence days of ozone, PM2.5 concentrations, air toxics cancer risk, and diesel particulate matter concentration. It enters the final GIPI equation directly.
Final GIPI Composite
Eq. 3 — “Classic” GIPI
GIPI = 0.6 · HC + 0.3 · HS + 0.1 · AQ
The “Classic” weight scheme (60 / 30 / 10) prioritizes hydrological risk, which is the most actionable target for green infrastructure. Heat severity receives secondary emphasis, while air quality serves as a supplementary factor.
